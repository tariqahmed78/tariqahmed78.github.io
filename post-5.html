Is 5GHz Wi-Fi Always Better Than 2.4GHz?

5 min read Published Jul 2026

When you set up a new router, you are almost always presented with two network bands: 2.4GHz and 5GHz. It's tempting to put every device on the 5GHz network assuming it's the "faster and better" option. But networking is about trade-offs, and bigger numbers aren't universally superior.

The 5GHz advantage: Speed and clarity

The 5GHz band is a wider, less congested highway. Because it supports faster data rates and doesn't share airspace with older microwaves, baby monitors, and Bluetooth devices, it can deliver incredible speeds. If you are downloading a large game update or streaming a 4K movie to your TV in the living room (where the router is), 5GHz is the absolute best choice.

The 2.4GHz advantage: Range and penetration

The problem with 5GHz is physics: higher frequency radio waves have a much harder time penetrating solid objects like brick walls, floors, and heavy furniture. This is where 2.4GHz shines. While its maximum speed is lower, its waves can travel significantly further and punch through walls much more effectively. If you're trying to browse the web on your phone from the backyard, or you have a smart security camera mounted outside the garage, 2.4GHz is often the only band that will provide a stable connection.

How to assign your devices

Use 5GHz for devices that demand high bandwidth and are physically close to the router without many walls in between — think smart TVs, gaming consoles, and your primary laptop. Use 2.4GHz for devices that are far away, behind multiple walls, or simply don't need much bandwidth, such as smart plugs, light bulbs, e-readers, and older smartphones.
